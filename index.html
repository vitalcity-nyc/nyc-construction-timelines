Conflicts

Where the rules conflict

Specific cases where different legal authorities create contradictory or compounding requirements.
1

DOB vs. FDNY: parallel fire safety review

Both agencies review fire protection systems under separate legal authorities. DOB reviews under the 2014 Building Code; FDNY reviews under 3 RCNY Chapter 9 and the NYC Fire Code. FDNY review is described as "concurrent" but operates sequentially in practice — FDNY will not schedule re-inspection until DOB has substantially approved plans. FDNY re-inspection backlog: approximately 7 weeks.

Building Code Ch. 9 (DOB) vs. 3 RCNY Ch. 9 (FDNY); Admin Code Title 29
2

MDL vs. Building Code: egress standards

Multiple Dwelling Law Section 187 (state law, 1929) requires two independent means of egress for most multifamily buildings, but allows a single means with sprinklers. Building Code Section 1006.3.2 (city law, 2014) already permits single exit up to 6 stories for apartments with sprinklers — but with a 2,000 sq ft per story cap that makes it viable only on very small lots. Expanding this requires amending both city and state law.

MDL Section 187 vs. NYC Building Code Ch. 10, Section 1006
3

City of Yes vs. DOB systems

The December 2024 City of Yes for Housing Opportunity amended the Zoning Resolution to eliminate parking mandates, legalize ADUs, and expand transit-oriented development. DOB plan examiners must now apply these new provisions, but DOB NOW's automated zoning-check modules have not been fully updated. Projects filed under the new rules may receive objections based on the old zoning parameters.

ZR amendments (Dec 2024) vs. DOB NOW system validation rules
4

Self-certification: speed without accountability

Admin Code 28-104.2.1 allows licensed professionals to self-certify code compliance, bypassing DOB review. But DOB does not always audit self-certified applications within the mandated 45 days, and the DOB NOW system lacks a self-certification tracking field — meaning the city cannot determine what share of current construction is self-certified.

Admin Code 28-104.2.1 and 28-104.2.1.1; NYC Comptroller audit (2023)
5

The 60th Street line

CEQR Technical Manual Chapter 16 uses 60th Street in Manhattan as a dividing line for traffic modeling: 50% taxi overlap below, 25% above. An identical project at 59th Street and 61st Street faces different environmental review standards. Below 60th Street, transit mode share exceeds 70% and car ownership is among the lowest in the US, yet traffic analysis is still required for projects generating 50+ peak-hour vehicle trips.

CEQR Technical Manual Ch. 16, Section 311
6

Live-in super: 1929 rule meets 2025 buildings

MDL Section 83 requires a resident superintendent for buildings with 13+ families where the owner does not reside. This 1929 rule was written for coal-boiler buildings with manual systems. Modern buildings with automated HVAC and remote monitoring still must dedicate a unit to the super, reducing housing supply by 5-8% in mid-size buildings. The MDL is state law — NYC cannot change it unilaterally.

Multiple Dwelling Law Section 83 (NYS, 1929)

Data sources and methodology

All figures are auditable. Here is where the data came from, how it was calculated, and what it cannot tell you.

All data was pulled from NYC Open Data Socrata APIs on April 1, 2026. Processing times calculated as the difference between filing_date and first_permit_date (DOB) or received_date and issue_date (LPC).

Processing time methodology: Calendar days between filing_date and first_permit_date for DOB filings, and received_date to issue_date for LPC applications. Records with negative/zero processing times were excluded, as were records exceeding 3,650 days.

Limitations:

  • DOB NOW migration (2020-2022) created a break in the time series
  • FDNY plan review has no public dataset; 7-week re-inspection backlog is from industry reports and the NYC Comptroller
  • Self-certification data is not available in DOB NOW
  • CO issuance data includes both TCOs and final COs
  • Processing times measure DOB internal review only; pre-filing (LPC, CEQR, ULURP) and post-permit (FDNY LOA, final CO) are separate

Regulatory citations: Current as of April 2026. Key authorities: NYC Admin Code Title 28 (Buildings), Title 29 (Fire), Title 25 Ch. 3 (Landmarks); NYC Building Code (2014, as amended); NYC Zoning Resolution; Multiple Dwelling Law (NYS); CEQR Technical Manual (2025 edition); NYC Charter Chapters 8, 26, 27, 45.
