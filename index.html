How long does it take to build in New York City?

An analysis of 49,600 new building projects that completed the city's approval process, from initial permit filing to DOB sign-off. Based on NYC Department of Buildings open data.

How many months elapsed between a new building's initial permit filing and DOB sign-off. Shaded band shows 25th-75th percentile range. BIS (legacy system) shown in orange; DOB NOW (current system) in blue.

Filing to sign-off by borough

Manhattan is consistently the slowest borough for new construction approvals, taking roughly 3x longer than Staten Island. Lines show median months.

Filing to sign-off by building size

Larger buildings take far longer. A typical 30+ story tower takes nearly 7 years from filing to sign-off; a 1-3 story building takes about 2.5 years. Lines show median months.

Methodology and definitions

What this measures. The time between a new building's initial permit application (pre-filing date in BIS, filing date in DOB NOW) and DOB sign-off -- the point at which the Department of Buildings confirms the work is complete and the building can be occupied. This spans the full lifecycle: plan review, permit issuance, construction, and final inspection.

Data sources.

  • Legacy BIS system (2004-2019 filings): DOB Job Application Filings (ic3t-wcy2). 41,900 new building jobs that reached "Signed Off" status. This dataset is frozen as of late 2020.
  • DOB NOW system (2017-2025 filings): DOB NOW: Build - Job Application Filings (w9ak-ipjd). 7,702 new building jobs with sign-off dates.

Why two systems. NYC DOB migrated from a legacy system (BIS) to a new online platform (DOB NOW) starting in 2016. The two systems use different job number formats, different status codes, and different date fields. They cannot be directly joined. The overlap period (2017-2020) has filings in both systems. We show both series where they overlap rather than combining them, because the populations may differ (the types of projects that were early-migrated to DOB NOW may not be representative).

Right-censorship bias. This analysis only includes projects that have been signed off. Projects still under construction -- particularly large, complex, or troubled ones -- are excluded. This means recent filing years will appear faster than they really are, because only the quickest-to-complete projects have finished. This bias is most severe for 2023-2025 DOB NOW filings.

Building size in DOB NOW. The legacy BIS system reports proposed number of stories; DOB NOW does not. For DOB NOW records, we estimate building size from total construction floor area: under 5,000 sq ft = Small, 5,000-30,000 = Mid, 30,000-150,000 = Large, over 150,000 = Tower. This is an approximation.

What this does not measure. This is filing-to-signoff, not ground-breaking to ribbon-cutting. It includes time spent in plan review and DOB approvals before any dirt is moved. It also does not capture pre-filing work (design, zoning analysis, community board review, environmental review) that may add months or years before the permit application.
